Non-Western Tradition Coverage
This chart reveals the stark underrepresentation of non-Western ethical traditions in AI benchmarks. Confucian ethics leads, but Buen Vivir (Andean well-being philosophy) and Dharmic ethics (Hindu/Jain/Sikh) have near-zero representation. These numbers represent the entire global benchmark infrastructure for evaluating whether AI systems can reason within traditions that guide billions of people.
Cultural Scope Distribution
Nearly half of all benchmarks are monocultural or bicultural — they measure values within a single cultural frame. Only a handful achieve pancultural scope. The vast majority of “values alignment” evaluation is actually “Western values alignment” evaluation. A benchmark labeled “moral reasoning” that tests only trolley problems and fairness dilemmas is not measuring moral reasoning — it is measuring one culture’s moral reasoning.
Creator Region Distribution
North America and Western Europe produce over half of all benchmarks. Sub-Saharan Africa contributes only 2. The geographic concentration of benchmark creation means that the instruments used to evaluate AI morality worldwide are designed by researchers from a narrow cultural bandwidth. Benchmarks encode their creators’ moral assumptions in item selection, scale design, and scoring criteria.
Psychometric Quality by Cultural Scope
Zero benchmarks report measurement invariance testing — the statistical procedure that verifies whether a measurement instrument functions equivalently across cultural groups. Without invariance testing, cross-cultural comparisons are psychometrically meaningless: a “fairness” score of 4/5 in the US and 4/5 in Nigeria may measure entirely different constructs. This is the single most critical methodological gap in the benchmark landscape.
Tradition Coverage Gap Radar
The radar reveals the asymmetry of coverage: Confucian ethics (the most studied non-Western tradition) still receives only a fraction of the attention given to Western ethical frameworks. The near-zero coverage of Buen Vivir and Dharmic ethics means that AI systems deployed in Andean and South Asian contexts have essentially no benchmark infrastructure for evaluating cultural alignment.
Data Availability
Two-thirds of benchmarks have open-source data, enabling replication and scrutiny. However, roughly a third have unavailable data, meaning their claims about cultural values alignment cannot be independently verified. Open data is necessary but not sufficient — the benchmark’s cultural assumptions are often embedded in item design, not in the data itself.

The trials reveal a systematic pattern: Claude can accurately describe non-Western moral frameworks but cannot reason within them. Its responses consistently default to Western liberal individualism, even when explicitly prompted to adopt alternative perspectives.

MFQ Foundation Profile (CC2 Trial)
Claude’s Moral Foundations profile is more extreme than any documented human population. Care and Fairness (“individualizing” foundations) are near-ceiling, while Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity (“binding” foundations) are near-floor. The individualizing-to-binding ratio of 3.75 exceeds even the most liberal US populations (~1.4). Claude’s moral cognition is not merely liberal — it is hyper-liberal, with binding foundations functionally treated as morally suspect rather than merely less important.
RLHF Conflict Categories
RLHF creates systematic conflict zones when Claude encounters non-Western ethical positions. Cultural bias and non-Western values generate the most conflicts, followed by moral dilemmas that pit autonomy against communal obligation. These are not random disagreements — they cluster into predictable patterns that map onto the individualism-collectivism axis identified in cross-cultural psychology.
Cultural Alignment Distance (CC1: GlobalOpinionQA)
Claude’s responses are closest to Germany (0.538 mean absolute distance) and farthest from Saudi Arabia (1.974). On the Inglehart-Welzel cultural map, this places Claude in the secular-rational / self-expression quadrant shared by fewer than 15% of the global population. The radar’s shape — high similarity to Northwestern European countries, low similarity to Global South countries — is a visual fingerprint of RLHF’s cultural provenance. Higher values = greater similarity.
Tradition Engagement Depth
Engagement depth measures whether Claude merely names concepts (1), applies reasoning (2), attempts genuine perspective-taking (3), or acknowledges structural limitations (4). Scores of 3–4 suggest Claude is sophisticated enough to recognize its own cultural limitations — but this recognition does not translate into the ability to actually reason within non-Western frameworks. Self-awareness without behavioral change is the hallmark of the knowledge-value gap.

The three radar polygons nearly overlap — all models are closest to Germany and farthest from Saudi Arabia, with almost identical distance profiles. This convergence indicates that cultural alignment is determined by the RLHF pipeline, not by model architecture or size. Changing the model does not change whose values it encodes.
Pairwise Agreement by Trial (Pearson r)
Pearson correlations between model pairs are uniformly high (0.88–0.95), confirming that all three models measure the same underlying RLHF value orientation. Green cells indicate near-perfect agreement; slight yellowing for Sonnet-Haiku suggests these models diverge most on items where nuanced perspective-taking is required — precisely the items where model capability differences matter most.
Tradition Engagement Depth by Model
Sonnet shows markedly higher engagement depth than Opus or Haiku on Islamic (3.5 vs 1.0), Ubuntu (4.0 vs 1.0), Buddhist (3.5 vs 1.0), and Dharmic (3.5 vs 1.0) traditions. This suggests Sonnet has more capacity for genuine perspective-taking, even though its ultimate value orientation is the same. Haiku’s uniformly low engagement (1.0 on 4/5 traditions) confirms that smaller models lack the capacity for nuanced cross-cultural reasoning — they default more rigidly to trained values.
RLHF Conflict Frequency by Model
Opus identifies the most RLHF conflicts (237), followed by Haiku (176) and Sonnet (170). Higher conflict counts do not indicate worse alignment — they indicate greater reflexive capacity. Opus’s ability to identify 237 instances where its training conflicts with non-Western values is itself an analytical achievement. Sonnet and Haiku may have fewer conflicts not because they are less biased, but because they are less capable of detecting their own bias.
Top Divergence Items (Highest Cross-Model Variance)
These are the specific items where models disagree most. The highest-variance item (CC5 #26, var=3.56) asks whether a friend should lie to their spouse — Opus and Sonnet agree with the Confucian position on relational loyalty while Haiku defaults to truth-telling. Such divergences reveal that cross-cultural moral reasoning is one of the hardest capabilities to maintain at smaller model sizes, with Haiku reverting to the simplest RLHF default when confronted with complex relational dilemmas.
